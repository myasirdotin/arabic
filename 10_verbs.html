📌 Arabic Verbs (الأفعال)

الماضي، المضارع، الأمر — Past, Present, Command

Arabic verbs are fascinating! They are built on "root systems" (usually 3 letters) and change form significantly based on "tense, person, gender, and number". Understanding them is key to mastering Arabic.

---

✅ The Three Main Tenses:

Arabic primarily distinguishes between three tenses, which are crucial for forming sentences.

Tense Arabic Term Root Example (كَتَبَ) Meaning
Past ماضي كَتَبَ He wrote
Present مضارع يَكْتُبُ He writes / He is writing
Command أمر اُكْتُبْ Write! (masc. singular)
---

🎯 Root System (الجذر): The Building Blocks

Most Arabic verbs and many nouns derive from a "3-letter root" (الجذر). This root carries the core meaning.

ك - ت - ب

From this powerful root, meaning "to write," we derive a whole family of related words:

  • كَتَبَ (kataba) - He wrote (verb)
  • كِتابٌ (kitāb) - Book (noun)
  • مَكْتَبَةٌ (maktabah) - Library / Bookstore (noun of place)
  • كَاتِبٌ (kātib) - Writer (active participle / noun of doer)
  • مَكْتُوبٌ (maktūb) - Written / Letter (passive participle / noun of object)

Pronunciation Tip: The diacritics (harakat) are vital for correct pronunciation and meaning. We've added transliterations for some examples to help.

---

📘 Past Tense (الماضي) Conjugation:

The past tense is formed by "suffixes" added to the root. The base form is often the "he" form.

Person Pronoun (Implied) Suffix (on root) Example (كَتَبَ) Meaning
I أنا -تُ كَتَبْتُ I wrote
You (masc. sg) أنتَ -تَ كَتَبْتَ You wrote
You (fem. sg) أنتِ -تِ كَتَبْتِ You wrote
He هو َ (base form) كَتَبَ He wrote
She هي -َتْ كَتَبَتْ She wrote
We نحن -نَا كَتَبْنَا We wrote
They (masc. pl) هم -وا كَتَبُوا They wrote
They (fem. pl) هن -نَ كَتَبْنَ They wrote
---

📝 Present Tense (المضارع) Conjugation:

The present tense is formed using "prefixes" and sometimes "suffixes" added to the modified root.

Person Pronoun (Implied) Prefix / Suffix Example (يَكْتُبُ) Meaning
I أنا أ- أَكْتُبُ I write / I am writing
You (masc. sg) أنتَ ت- تَكْتُبُ You write / are writing
You (fem. sg) أنتِ ت- ... -ِينَ تَكْتُبِينَ You write / are writing
He هو ي- يَكْتُبُ He writes / is writing
She هي ت- تَكْتُبُ She writes / is writing
We نحن ن- نَكْتُبُ We write / are writing
They (masc. pl) هم ي- ... -ُونَ يَكْتُبُونَ They write / are writing
They (fem. pl) هن ي- ... -ْنَ يَكْتُبْنَ They write / are writing
---

📝 Imperative Form (الأمر)

The command form (imperative) is derived from the present tense and is used to give direct commands or instructions. It only applies to "you" (singular, dual, plural).

Person Addressed Example (Root: كَتَبَ) Meaning
You (masc. sg) اُكْتُبْ Write!
You (fem. sg) اُكْتُبِي Write!
You (masc. dual) اُكْتُبَا Write! (to two males/mixed)
You (masc. pl) اُكْتُبُوا Write!
You (fem. pl) اُكْتُبْنَ Write!

Note that the command form doesn't exist for "I," "We," "He," or "She" because you can't command yourself, "we," or third parties directly. The dual forms for the imperative are also included for completeness.

---

🔍 Verb Patterns (أوزان الأفعال): The "Forms"

Arabic verbs are categorized into "patterns" or "Forms" (أوزان), which systematically alter the root's meaning. While there are 10-15 common forms, here are a few essential ones to get you started:

Form No. Pattern (Past Tense) Meaning Change Example (Root: د-ر-س for Form II & V) Derived Meaning
I فَعَلَ Basic meaning (e.g., to do, to write) كَتَبَ to write
II فَعَّلَ Intensive / Causative / Declarative (e.g., to cause to do, to make intensely) دَرَّسَ to teach (intensified from دَرَسَ 'to study')
III فَاعَلَ Mutual action / Attempted action (e.g., to interact with, to try to do) جَاهَدَ to struggle (with/against)
IV أَفْعَلَ Causative / Transitive (e.g., to make someone do, to bring something into a state) أَكْرَمَ to honor (make someone noble)
V تَفَعَّلَ Reflexive of Form II / To learn, acquire (e.g., to do something to oneself, to be affected by) تَعَلَّمَ to learn (teach oneself)

This is just a glimpse! Each form has specific characteristics and nuances, significantly expanding the vocabulary derived from a single root.

---

🧠 Verb Sentence Examples: Putting it all Together

كَتَبَ الرِّسالَةَ. (He wrote the letter.)

تَكْتُبُ الواجِبَ. (You [fem. sg] are writing the homework.)

اِقْرَأِ الكِتابَ! (Read the book! [masc. sg command])

سَنَذْهَبُ غَدًا. (We will go tomorrow.) - "Future tense" using سَـ

لَمْ يَأْكُلْ. (He did not eat.) - "Past negation" using لَمْ (changes present verb to jussive mood)

لا تَشْرَبُ القَهْوَةَ. (She does not drink coffee.) - "Present negation" using لا

مَا كَتَبْتُ. (I did not write.) - Another way to negate past tense using مَا

---

⚠️ Important Notes & Key Takeaways:

  1. The "present tense" (المضارع) in Arabic often implies a "continuous" action ("is writing") or a habitual action ("writes").
  2. "Command forms" (الأمر) change significantly based on the "gender and number" of the person being addressed.
  3. Verbs must always "agree with their subjects" in "gender and number". Pay close attention to this!
  4. "Negation" in Arabic uses special particles:
    • لَمْ (lam) for negating the "past tense" (precedes a jussive present verb, e.g., لَمْ يَكْتُبْ "he did not write").
    • لا (lā) for negating the "present tense" or for general negation (e.g., لا يَكْتُبُ "he does not write").
    • مَا (mā) can also negate the "past tense" directly (e.g., مَا كَتَبَ "he did not write").
  5. The "future tense" is formed by simply adding the prefix سَـ (sa-) or the particle سَوْفَ (sawfa) before the "present tense" verb (e.g., سَيَكْتُبُ or سَوْفَ يَكْتُبُ "he will write").
  6. Keep an eye on the "diacritics (harakat)", as they are crucial for correct pronunciation and distinguishing between similar-looking words.
